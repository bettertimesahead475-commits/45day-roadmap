Days 21–45

This stage focuses on preparing for court, demonstrating progress, and strengthening your position.

1. Continue All Services Consistently

CAS and the court look for consistency. Missing sessions can be used against you, so stay committed.

2. Strengthen Your Documentation

  • Track all access visits
  • Record service attendance
  • Save all emails with CAS
  • Keep notes of all meetings

Good documentation helps your lawyer advocate for you.

3. Prepare for Court

Work with your lawyer to understand:

  • Your position
  • CAS’s position
  • What the court will expect
  • What outcomes are possible

4. Maintain Strong Access Visits

Visits are one of the strongest indicators of your relationship with your child. Stay consistent, calm, and child‑focused.

5. Avoid Common Mistakes

  • Missing appointments
  • Sending emotional messages
  • Arguing with workers
  • Not following through on commitments

Small mistakes can slow your progress — stay focused.

1. Review Disclosure Carefully

Go through the disclosure with your lawyer. Identify inaccuracies, missing context, or concerns.

2. Begin Services Immediately

CAS often recommends services such as:

  • Parenting programs
  • Counselling
  • Addictions support (if relevant)
  • Mental health services

Starting early shows initiative and responsibility.

3. Attend All Access Visits

Consistency is critical. Arrive early, stay calm, and stay child‑focused.

4. Communicate Professionally

Keep all communication with CAS:

  • Brief
  • Respectful
  • Written (email preferred)

Never send emotional or confrontational messages.

5. Prepare for the Case Conference

This is often the first major court‑related meeting. Work with your lawyer to prepare your position and goals.

Information only — not legal advice.

1. Motions (If They Happen)

A motion is a request for the court to make a temporary decision. Common motions include:

  • Changes to access (more time, supervised to unsupervised, etc.)
  • Requests for assessments
  • Requests for specific services
  • Concerns about safety or compliance

Your lawyer will guide you through whether a motion is necessary or how to respond to one.

2. Negotiations and Agreements

Many cases move toward agreements during this period. These may include:

  • Updated access schedules
  • Service plan adjustments
  • Timelines for next steps
  • Expectations for both you and CAS

Agreements can help avoid unnecessary conflict and keep the case moving forward.

3. Strengthening Your Documentation

Documentation becomes extremely important during this phase. Keep organized records of:

  • Access visits (dates, times, activities, positives)
  • Service attendance (proof, confirmations, certificates)
  • Communication with CAS (emails, messages)
  • Any progress you’ve made

Good documentation helps your lawyer advocate for you and shows the court your commitment.

4. Reviewing Your Progress

CAS and the court will look at whether you have:

  • Engaged in services consistently
  • Attended all access visits
  • Communicated respectfully
  • Followed recommendations
  • Shown stability and effort

Even small steps matter — progress is progress.

5. Preparing for the Next Court Date

By the end of the first 45 days, you should be prepared for the next major court appearance. This may include:

  • Updated disclosure
  • Service updates
  • Access reports
  • CAS recommendations
  • Your lawyer’s plan moving forward

Being organized and prepared helps your lawyer advocate more effectively.

Important

Do not stop services. Do not miss visits. Do not send emotional messages to CAS. Consistency during this period is one of the strongest indicators of long‑term success in your case.
