Working With CAS

How to communicate, document, and navigate your relationship with the Children’s Aid Society.

1. Keep Communication Professional

Always communicate in a calm, respectful, and brief manner. Avoid emotional or confrontational messages.

  • Use email whenever possible
  • Stick to facts, not feelings
  • Avoid long explanations

2. Document Everything

Good documentation protects you and helps your lawyer advocate for you.

  • Save all emails
  • Record dates and times of calls
  • Write down what was said
  • Keep copies of all CAS documents

3. Attend All Meetings

Missing meetings can be used against you. Show up early, stay calm, and take notes.

4. Follow Through on Commitments

If you agree to do something, do it. CAS and the court look closely at reliability and consistency.

5. Ask for Information in Writing

Written communication creates a clear record and reduces misunderstandings.

CAS plays a major role in your case. How you communicate and cooperate with them can influence access, timelines, and the overall direction of your file. You don’t have to agree with everything — but you do need to stay calm, organized, and consistent.

1. Communicating With CAS

Good communication helps your case. Keep it:

  • Respectful — even when you disagree
  • Brief — stick to the point
  • Written — email is best for records
  • Calm — avoid emotional messages

If something is important, ask for it in writing.

2. Document Everything

Keep organized records of:

  • Emails and messages with CAS
  • Access visits (dates, times, activities)
  • Service attendance (proof, confirmations)
  • Any concerns or corrections

Documentation protects you and helps your lawyer advocate for you.

3. Asking for Clarification

If you don’t understand something CAS says or requests, ask:

  • “Can you clarify what you mean?”
  • “Can you send that in writing?”
  • “What is the timeline for this?”

Clear communication prevents misunderstandings.

4. When You Disagree

You can disagree respectfully. Try:

  • “I understand your concern, but I see it differently.”
  • “Can we review the information together?”
  • “I’d like to discuss this with my lawyer.”

Never argue or send angry messages — it can be used against you.

5. Showing Progress

CAS looks for consistent effort, such as:

  • Attending services regularly
  • Showing up early to access visits
  • Communicating respectfully
  • Following through on recommendations

Small steps add up and show responsibility.

Important

Do not ignore CAS messages. Do not send emotional or confrontational responses. Do not refuse services without speaking to your lawyer first.
