Days 7–21

The middle phase of the first 45 days: case conferences, access visits, service plans, and showing progress.

Why Days 7–21 Matter

This is where CAS and the court start forming a clearer picture of your situation. Your consistency, communication, and willingness to engage in services during this period can strongly influence how the case moves forward.

1. The Case Conference

The case conference is usually the first major meeting with a judge. It is not a trial. The purpose is to:

  • Identify the main issues in the case.
  • Discuss possible solutions or agreements.
  • Review progress you’ve made so far.
  • Set timelines for next steps.

Judges often give guidance here. Showing cooperation and preparation makes a strong impression.

2. Strengthening Your Access Visits

If your child is not in your care, access visits are one of the most important parts of your case. During Days 7–21, CAS and the court look closely at:

  • Your consistency — showing up early and every time.
  • Your interactions — positive, child‑focused, calm.
  • Your preparation — bringing activities, snacks, or comfort items.
  • Your documentation — keeping notes after each visit.

Positive access visits help demonstrate stability and readiness.

3. Engaging in Services

By this stage, CAS will usually recommend specific services. Even if you disagree with the concerns, starting services shows responsibility and commitment. Common services include:

  • Parenting programs
  • Counselling or therapy
  • Addictions support (if relevant)
  • Anger management
  • Domestic violence programs (if relevant)

Keep proof of attendance — certificates, emails, appointment confirmations, or screenshots.

4. Communication With CAS

During this period, CAS is watching for:

  • Respectful communication
  • Timely responses
  • Willingness to cooperate
  • Effort to follow recommendations

Ask for important information in writing. Keep all emails and messages organized.

5. Reviewing the Service Plan

CAS will often provide a service plan outlining what they want you to work on. You do not have to agree with everything, but you should:

  • Read it carefully.
  • Ask questions if anything is unclear.
  • Start working on the items you can control.
  • Document your progress.

Showing effort early can influence access, timelines, and future decisions.

Important

Do not argue with CAS workers. Do not send emotional messages. Do not miss services or visits. Your consistency during this period is one of the strongest predictors of how the case will move forward.
